You are designing a mobile-first web app for Harvard College international undergraduates, especially low-income students and students from the African continent on full financial aid. The app has two main phases:

Pre‑arrival: an inclusive, safe information‑sharing and community space
Post‑arrival: an in‑person interactive scavenger hunt to get to know campus resources, spaces, and people
The goal is to reduce isolation, demystify the hidden curriculum, and help international students feel prepared, welcomed, and connected.

Target users
Primary: Incoming Harvard College international students (particularly low-income and/or first‑generation), many from educational systems emphasizing rote memorization, limited group work, and little exposure to U.S. classroom norms.
Secondary: Peer mentors (upper‑class international and domestic students), proctors/Residential tutors, staff at the international office and related programs.
Core problems to address (from research)
Academic transition & hidden curriculum

Students are underprepared for U.S. teaching style (participation, p‑sets, collaboration).
Don’t understand “hidden curriculum” around:
Course selection strategy (e.g., back‑to‑back classes, workload balance).
Getting internships/summer opportunities (especially with visa constraints).
Navigating funding, research, and competitive programs.
U.S.-specific references in class (politics, pop culture, economics examples) create barriers.
Basic needs & logistics

Confusion around housing during winter/spring breaks and summer.
Lack of clear information on funding for summer, visa limits on work, and what “full financial aid” does/does not cover (laundry, toiletries, daily essentials).
Stress around finding summer housing and jobs/internships in the U.S.
Social integration & extracurriculars

Students struggle to join clubs; competitive club culture feels exclusionary.
Many international students must work on‑campus jobs, leaving less time for social life.
Limited mixing between international and domestic students; missed cultural exchange.
Well‑being & mental health

Orientation mentions mental health resources, but students are:
Overwhelmed at first and don’t use them when they most need them.
Uncomfortable sharing with strangers; worried about worrying their families.
Support drops sharply after the first few weeks; international pre‑orientation (like FIP) is helpful but not sustained.
Visa, jobs, and career support

Faculty and staff often don’t understand visa limits and international job search realities.
Career centers lack clearly relevant opportunities and guidance for international students.
Students have to self‑navigate the U.S. job market and networking norms.
Overall product vision
Design an app that:

Before arrival

Provides a psychologically safe, low‑pressure space where incoming international students can:
Ask “basic” or sensitive questions anonymously or semi‑anonymously.
Learn from curated, trustworthy content and lived experiences of current students.
Understand academic norms, hidden curriculum, and basic logistical realities early.
Reduces anxiety by clarifying what to expect in academics, social life, finances, jobs, and culture.
After arrival

Turns orientation and the first month into a fun, interactive scavenger hunt (similar to GooseChase) that:
Physically takes students to key Harvard spaces and resources (e.g., international office, health & counseling, financial aid office, libraries, writing center, career services, dining halls, community centers, etc.).
Facilitates in‑person connections with other international students and domestic peers.
Rewards curiosity, collaboration, and exploration rather than competition.
Phase 1: Pre‑arrival space (digital, remote)
Core features
Onboarding & profiles

Simple onboarding with:
Name (optional display name / nickname).
Country/region, time zone.
Intended concentration (optional).
Comfort levels: speaking in groups, asking questions publicly, meeting new people.
Option to use a pseudonym in group spaces to reduce anxiety.
Themed “Safe Rooms” (channels or spaces)

Create topic‑based discussion spaces where incoming students can post or browse:
“Academics & Learning Style”
“Hidden Curriculum & Class Planning”
“Money, Housing, & Everyday Life”
“Jobs, Internships, & Visas”
“Mental Health & Well‑Being”
“Social Life, Clubs, & Making Friends”
“U.S. Culture & Harvard Culture”
In each space, users can:
Post questions (with anonymous posting toggle).
React/like and reply.
Tag posts with sub‑topics (e.g., “econ,” “STEM p‑sets,” “winter break,” “club comps”).
Peer‑validated answers and stories

Allow verified upper‑class international students and trained peer mentors to:
Mark answers as “Helpful” or “Verified from experience.”
Share short stories (e.g., “What I wish I knew before my first exam,” “How I found summer housing,” “My first club comp horror story and what I learned.”).
Highlight multiple perspectives, not just official “rules.”
Curated resources distilled from Harvard’s existing info

Create short, digestible guides (cards or FAQs) that translate official info into student‑friendly language, with examples:
“How to read the course catalog & plan your schedule without burning out.”
“Intro to p‑sets & collaboration norms (what’s allowed, what’s not).”
“Understanding financial aid, hidden costs (laundry, toiletries, flights), and what’s covered.”
“Break housing: what happens during winter and spring; what options exist.”
“Summer funding and internships for internationals: timelines & strategies.”
“Mental health: what it actually looks like to use counseling at Harvard.”
Include search across guides and Q&A.
Light‑touch social connection before arrival

Features that avoid pressure but build familiarity:
Country/region‑based groups (e.g., “Students from East Africa”) and interest‑based groups (e.g., “First‑gen / low‑income,” “STEM,” “Humanities & Arts”).
Optional small “buddy circles” (3–6 students) auto‑matched around common interests/time zones, with low‑commitment icebreaker prompts.
Async prompts like: “Share one thing you’re excited about and one thing you’re worried about.”
Moderation, safety, and tone

Clear community guidelines emphasizing:
Respect for accents, backgrounds, and different educational systems.
Non‑judgmental, no‑shame questions.
Basic reporting tools for posts or replies.
Human moderators (peer mentors/staff) have simple dashboards to:
Pin important posts.
Remove harmful content.
Send gentle reminders or supportive messages.
Personalized “prep path”

After onboarding, generate a personalized checklist/timeline:
Academic: watch/read 1–2 pieces about U.S. classroom norms, p‑sets, office hours.
Life logistics: learn about break housing and daily costs.
Mental health: a gentle intro to support options and real student testimonials.
Include optional micro‑tasks (short videos, quizzes, reflection prompts) to make it interactive but not burdensome.
Phase 2: On‑campus interactive scavenger hunt
This phase starts once students are physically on campus and builds on the relationships and knowledge from Phase 1.

Core concept
An app‑based mission system (like GooseChase) where students or small teams complete location‑based and interaction‑based challenges that guide them to key Harvard spaces and people, with rewards that are fun and social rather than purely competitive.

Key design goals
Help international students:
Physically locate and feel comfortable entering key resource offices and community spaces.
Practice low‑stakes social interactions with peers, staff, and domestic students.
Learn hidden curriculum tips embedded in missions.
Normalize help‑seeking (academic, financial, mental health).
Core features
Teams & participation options

Students can:
Join auto‑assigned mixed teams (international + domestic peers where possible).
Or form their own team with friends they met online or at orientation.
Also allow a solo mode for shy students, with missions tailored to gentle, non‑embarrassing tasks.
Mission types (examples)
Design mission templates the app can support:

Resource discovery missions

Example: “Visit the Office of International Education / International Office. Take a photo of something that surprised you or that you think will be important this year. Write one question you might ask them in the future.”
“Find the financial aid office. Ask anonymously or in person: ‘What should an international student do if they’re worried about money for breaks or summer?’ Summarize one tip.”
Academic support missions

“Go to the writing center or a tutoring space. Snap a photo of the entrance and record one myth about academic help that’s not true.”
“Find where your intro classes will be held. With a teammate, practice asking: ‘Could you clarify this concept?’ and type one sentence you’d say to a professor in office hours.”
Mental health and well‑being missions

“Locate the counseling and mental health services building. Without taking intrusive photos, capture a symbolic image (e.g., a sign, a tree) and write one reason someone might come here that is totally normal.”
“Go to a green space/walking path on campus. Reflect in 1–2 sentences on one self‑care habit you want to keep.”
Cultural & social integration missions

“Attend or visit a student organization fair or a club meeting. Take a picture of one club that seems interesting and share why, even if you’re not sure you’ll join.”
“Introduce yourself to one domestic student and ask them: ‘What is one thing you wish international students knew about life here?’ Record their advice (with consent) in text form.”
Hidden curriculum missions

“Ask a sophomore or junior: ‘What’s one mistake you made with course selection your first semester?’ Summarize their advice.”
“Find a spot where students normally do p‑sets. Capture an image of the space and write one question you still have about collaboration / academic honesty.”
Game mechanics

Points for:
Completing missions (more points for missions that involve talking to someone or visiting key support services).
Reflective answers, not just taking photos.
Badges for:
“Resource Explorer” – visited X support offices.
“Community Connector” – interacted with Y peers/staff.
“Hidden Curriculum Detective” – completed missions about course planning, jobs, etc.
Option for team leaderboards, but emphasize collaborative and completion‑based rewards over pure competition.
Small, tangible or symbolic rewards (if integrated with staff): lunch with peer mentors, swag, certificates.
Accessibility & inclusion in scavenger design

Ensure missions:
Do not require late‑night activity or long off‑campus travel (sensitive to safety & cost).
Have alternative versions for students with mobility, sensory, or mental health needs.
Avoid culturally insensitive or embarrassing tasks (no forced public performance or accent‑mocking).
Integration with pre‑arrival data

Use optional interest/concern data from Phase 1 to recommend missions:
If a student expressed high anxiety about finances, prioritize missions that connect them to financial aid and funding offices.
If anxious about mental health, show gentle missions that introduce counseling resources and peer‑support communities.
Post‑hunt reflection and continuity

After the hunt period (e.g., first 2–4 weeks of term), the app:
Prompts short reflections: “Which place/person felt most approachable? Least approachable? What still feels confusing?”
Surfaces ongoing events or programs from those offices.
Encourages students to stay in buddy circles or small groups established during the hunt.
Non‑functional requirements & experience goals
Tone & UX

Warm, informal, non‑judgmental language.
Emphasize that confusion is normal and everyone is figuring things out.
Use simple English and avoid U.S.-specific slang without explanation.
Privacy & safety

Allow pseudonyms and anonymous posting in Phase 1.
Control visibility of photos and content from scavenger hunt (e.g., only visible to team or within the app community).
Clear consent prompts for sharing other people’s quotes or images.
Admin/mentor tools

Simple dashboards for staff/mentors to:
Post announcements and time‑sensitive info (e.g., deadlines, visa info sessions).
Create/modify missions and activate them for specific cohorts.
View anonymized engagement stats to identify where students are struggling (e.g., many questions about housing, mental health).
Scalability

Designed so it can later support other cohorts (non‑African regions, grad students) with minimal configuration changes:
Customizable channels/topics.
Editable mission templates and resource guides.
Using all of the above, design the complete information architecture, main user flows, and interface elements for this app. Prioritize:

Reducing anxiety and isolation.
Surfacing hidden curriculum knowledge.
Encouraging sustainable, meaningful in‑person connections between international and domestic students.
Smooth transition from pre‑arrival digital space to on‑campus interactive scavenger hunt.
Output:

A clear breakdown of screens/pages and navigation.
Key data models (users, groups, questions, missions, submissions, badges).
Example content for at least 5 pre‑arrival guides and 10 scavenger hunt missions.
Suggestions for lightweight gamification and moderation that preserve psychological safety.
